# Principles of reasoning and argumentation - Cross-domain taxonomy

## Global classification of principles

| Cluster                                      | Focus question                                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ─ *Inferential validity* ─                   |                                                                                                                                 |
| Formal and Structural Integrity of Inference | Logical architecture of arguments: elimination of redundancy, locality of inferential steps, and formal correctness             |
| Relevance and Premise Discipline             | Entitlement of premises to participate in an inference: each premise must be genuinely used and genuinely pertinent             |
| Epistemic Calibration                        | Proportionality between evidential warrant and claimed epistemic strength                                                       |
| Robustness and Defeasibility                 | Resilience of conclusions to challenges, objections, and defeaters                                                              |
| ─ *Explanatory adequacy* ─                   |                                                                                                                                 |
| Explanatory Power and Depth                  | Distinction between a proof that certifies truth and an argument that reveals why a conclusion holds necessarily                |
| Understanding Beyond Knowledge               | Transition from knowing-that to understanding-why: grasping modal, dependence, and structural relations                         |
| ─ *Communicability* ─                        |                                                                                                                                 |
| Discourse Organization and Transparency      | How reasoning is structured for reconstruction, navigation, and step-by-step intelligibility                                    |
| Dialectical and Contextual Norms             | Social, procedural, and context-dependent dimensions of argumentation                                                           |
| ─ *Cognitive uptake* ─                       |                                                                                                                                 |
| Cognitive Architecture and Processing        | How arguments interface with the structural constraints of human cognition: mental simulation, chunking, and analogical mapping |
| Knowledge Construction and Transfer          | Sequencing, scaffolding, and generalization of reasoning across contexts                                                        |

## Taxonomy with extracted principles

### I — Formal and Structural Integrity of Inference

| Principle                                    | Source field                                                   | Core content                                                                                                                                                                            |
| -------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| [**Canonical form**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^canonical-normal-form)                           | Proof theory (Gentzen, Prawitz)                                | Arguments must be reducible to a normal form in which every step is a direct application of introduction or elimination rules, with no detours.                                         |
| [**Detour elimination**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^detour-elimination) (subformula property) | Proof theory (Gentzen)                                         | Every intermediate proposition must be a subformula of the premises or conclusion; no external proposition should be imported and then eliminated.                                      |
| [**Introduction-rule primacy**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^introduction-rule-primacy)                | Proof theory (Prawitz)                                         | The meaning of each logical constant must be grounded in its introduction conditions; use must be preceded by canonical construction.                                                   |
| [**Complexity audit**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^complexity-audit)                         | Proof theory (Cook & Reckhow, Kreisel)                         | Disproportionate proof size relative to the conclusion is evidence of an indirect route, which suppresses structure that a more direct argument would reveal.                           |
| [**Explicit inferential structure**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^explicit-inferential-structure)           | Argumentation theory (Toulmin)                                 | The inferential articulation must be displayed explicitly: data, warrant, backing, qualifier, rebuttal, and claim constitute distinct components with distinct epistemic loads.         |
| [**Backing specification**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^backing-specification)                    | Argumentation theory (Toulmin)                                 | The warrant itself requires backing: the domain-specific body of knowledge that justifies its applicability.                                                                            |
| [**Quantifier and scope explicitness**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^quantifier-scope-explicitness)        | Dialogical logic (Hintikka)                                    | The order of quantifier choices, conditionals, and presuppositions is semantically critical; all scope dependencies must be made fully explicit.                                        |
| [**Local–global articulation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^local-global-articulation)                | Philosophy of mathematical explanation (Mancosu, Grothendieck) | Arguments must distinguish between local proofs (why this particular conclusion holds) and global theory-building (situating the conclusion within a broader theoretical architecture). |

### II — Relevance and Premise Discipline

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Relevance of premises**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^relevance-of-premises)|Proof theory (Gentzen, Anderson & Belnap)|Every premise must be actually used in deriving the conclusion; the structural rule of weakening must not be permitted to generate inferentially idle hypotheses.|
|[**Evidential relevance as probabilistic dependence**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^evidential-relevance)|Bayesian epistemology (Haenni, Hunter)|A premise is genuinely relevant if and only if it induces a non-trivial credence update: $P(C \mid E) \neq P(C)$.|
|[**Relevance of premises to conclusion**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^relevance-premises-conclusion)|Informal logic (Johnson & Blair)|Premises must be genuinely pertinent to the conclusion; ornamental lemmas or strategically inert remarks must be eliminated.|
|[**Explanatory relevance**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^explanatory-relevance)|Philosophy of science (Salmon, Woodward)|Explanation requires selecting factors that make a difference to the phenomenon; background conditions must be distinguished from explanatory drivers.|
|[**Sufficiency of support**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^sufficiency-of-support)|Informal logic (Johnson & Blair)|Premises must provide enough support; inferential jumps without intermediate bridges are inadmissible.|
|[**Acceptability of premises**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^acceptability-premises)|Informal logic (Johnson & Blair)|Premises must be entitled to function as support; dubious, undefended, equivocal, or illicit assumptions must be explicitly marked or eliminated.|

### III — Epistemic Calibration

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Proportionality of credence shift**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^proportionality-credence-shift)|Bayesian epistemology (Joyce, van Fraassen)|The degree of confidence attached to a conclusion must be proportional to the cumulative evidential support provided; unjustified inferential leaps are inadmissible.|
|[**Calibrating confidence**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^calibrating-confidence)|Bayesian epistemology (Joyce, Williamson)|Weak evidence licenses limited commitment; strong evidence licenses stronger commitment; residual uncertainty must not be suppressed.|
|[**Justification tracking**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^justification-tracking)|Epistemology of knowledge (Nozick, Sosa, Williamson)|A claim must not appear with more epistemic authority than its support warrants; modal strength must be calibrated: possible, plausible, supported, established, necessary.|
|[**Burden-of-proof discipline**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^burden-of-proof-discipline)|Argumentation theory (Walton)|Strong, surprising, universal, or normatively loaded claims impose heavier justificatory demands; the depth of justification must be calibrated to the strength of the claim.|
|[**Independence of evidence**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^independence-of-evidence)|Bayesian epistemology (Bovens & Hartmann)|When multiple pieces of evidence are assembled, their conditional independence given the hypothesis must be examined; correlated evidence sharing a common cause provides less cumulative support.|
|[**Prior sensitivity analysis**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^prior-sensitivity-analysis)|Bayesian epistemology (Levi, Berger, Hunter)|An argument must be robust across a range of reasonable priors; sensitivity to contested prior choices must be explicitly acknowledged.|

### IV — Robustness and Defeasibility

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Rebuttal pre-emption**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^rebuttal-pre-emption)|Argumentation theory (Toulmin)|Every argument must explicitly map its rebuttal space — conditions under which the inference fails or the claim does not hold.|
|[**Dialectical robustness**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^dialectical-robustness)|Argumentation theory (van Eemeren & Grootendorst)|A robust argument must treat objections, alternatives, counterexamples, and defeaters; it must distinguish decisive from merely prima facie support.|
|[**Sensitivity to defeaters**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^sensitivity-to-defeaters)|Epistemology of knowledge (Pollock, Reiter, Hunter & Thimm)|The threshold of defeating evidence at which the conclusion should be abandoned must be specified; the argument's conclusion must be assessed for degradation under rebutting or undercutting evidence.|
|[**Strategic completeness**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^strategic-completeness)|Dialogical logic (Lorenzen–Lorenz, Hintikka)|An argument must constitute a complete winning strategy, not merely a sequence of locally correct moves; each step must be chosen in awareness of all adversarial challenges.|
|[**Coherence of the belief system**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^coherence-belief-system)|Epistemology of knowledge (BonJour, Lehrer)|The claims must form a coherent network; later claims must be tested for compatibility with earlier assumptions.|

### V — Explanatory Power and Depth

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Why- and How-questions**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^why-how-questions)|Philosophy of science (Bromberger, van Fraassen)|A discourse produces understanding only when organized around a precise explanatory target relative to a contrast class, a relevance relation, and a body of background knowledge.|
|[**Causal and mechanistic articulation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^causal-mechanistic-articulation)|Philosophy of science (Salmon, Machamer, Craver, Woodward)|Understanding is enhanced when the discourse identifies the process, mechanism, or causal organization that generates the result, specifying entities, relations, and operations.|
|[**Necessity structure**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^necessity-structure)|Philosophy of science (Hempel & Oppenheim, DN model)|A discourse produces understanding when it reveals why a phenomenon is nomologically expected — derivable under laws and antecedent conditions — rather than merely actual.|
|[**Characteristic property dependence**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^characteristic-property-dependence)|Philosophy of mathematical explanation (Steiner)|An explanatory proof must identify an essential property of the central entity, make this property do the critical inferential work, and be structured so that varying the property varies the conclusion.|
|[**Unification**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^unification)|Philosophy of mathematical explanation (Kitcher, Friedman)|Understanding grows when many phenomena are derivable from a small set of principles or argument schemata; structural compression of disparate facts into a common architecture constitutes genuine explanatory gain.|
|[**Explanatory asymmetry**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^explanatory-asymmetry-science)|Philosophy of science and mathematical explanation (Lange, Bromberger, Salmon)|Some proofs reveal because-relations rather than mere material conditionals; the direction of the argument must respect the underlying generative priority and asymmetric dependency structure.|
|[**Symmetry and invariance exploitation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^symmetry-invariance-exploitation)|Philosophy of mathematical explanation (Lange, van Fraassen)|When a conclusion holds because of an underlying symmetry or invariance, the argument must make this explicit; such arguments are paradigm cases of explanatory asymmetry.|
|[**Counterfactual depth**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^counterfactual-depth)|Philosophy of science (Woodward, Lewis)|A powerful explanation supports answers to counterfactual questions: what would change if a factor were removed, which variations preserve the result, which perturbations destroy it.|

### VI — Understanding Beyond Knowledge

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Grasp of dependence relations**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^grasp-dependence-relations)|Epistemology of understanding (Kvanvig, Grimm)|Understanding consists in grasping the dependence and modal relations among facts, not merely in possessing a set of true beliefs.|
|[**Counterfactual robustness**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^counterfactual-robustness)|Epistemology of understanding (Grimm, Zagzebski, de Regt)|An argument must support what-if-things-had-been-different reasoning: identifying which modifications to premises or conditions would alter the conclusion, and how.|
|[**Intelligibility criterion**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^intelligibility-criterion)|Epistemology of understanding (de Regt)|An argument must enable recognition of qualitatively correct behavior without full re-derivation; it must allow prediction of the direction of variation under perturbation.|
|[**Minimization of epistemic opacity**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^minimization-epistemic-opacity)|Epistemology of understanding (Avigad, de Regt)|Each inferential step must be individually scrutable; black-box results must either be analyzed in place or explicitly flagged as assumed modules with deferred justification.|
|[**Computational affordances**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^computational-affordances)|Philosophy of mathematical explanation (Avigad)|Understanding a proof provides inferential affordances beyond its conclusion: navigating it, extracting structure, recognizing reusable methods, and supporting further inference.|
|[**Ability to transfer, reorganize and extend**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^transfer-reorganize-extend)|Epistemology of understanding (Zagzebski, Hills)|Understanding-why differs from knowing-why by the ability to answer a range of wh-questions from a position of insight, not merely to reproduce the derivation.|
|[**Coherence integration**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^coherence-integration)|Epistemology of understanding (Kvanvig)|Each claim must be explicitly connected to others through coherence relations — explanatory, inferential, contrastive, or evidential.|
|[**Appropriate abstraction**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^appropriate-abstraction)|Epistemology of understanding (Kvanvig, Grimm)|The level of abstraction must be chosen so that the decisive dependencies become intelligible: not so fine-grained as to drown the structure, not so coarse as to dissolve the real generating relations.|

### VII — Discourse Organization and Transparency

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Procedural transparency**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^reconstructibility-adversary)|Dialogical logic (Lorenzen & Lorenz, Barth & Krabbe)|An argument must be formulated so that a rational, informed opponent could reconstruct every move; implicit steps and unexplained leaps are inadmissible.|
|[**Incremental semantic construction**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^incremental-semantic-construction)|Dialogical logic|Each move in a discourse must carry identifiable semantic content; large leaps whose intermediate states have no interpretable meaning are structurally defective.|
|[**Framing and orientation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^framing-orientation)|Functional rhetoric (Goffman, Entman)|A discourse becomes intelligible when the target problem, the chosen strategy, and the role of each section within the whole are stated from the outset.|
|[**Salience hierarchy**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^salience-hierarchy)|Functional rhetoric (Perelman)|A discourse must mark hierarchical emphasis — main idea, decisive step, subordinate detail, optional remark — so that the backbone is visible and peripheral details do not mask the core.|
|[**Concession tracking**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^concession-tracking)|Dialogical logic (Barth & Krabbe, Hamblin, Walton & Krabbe)|An explicit record of what has been conceded by each party at each stage must be maintained; implicitly conceded propositions must be acknowledged.|
|[**Distinction of dialectical roles**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^distinction-dialectical-roles)|Argumentation theory (van Eemeren & Grootendorst, Walton)|Each stage of a reasoning sequence must have a clear discursive role: advancing a standpoint, challenging, defending, conceding, withdrawing a claim.|
|[**Progressive disclosure**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^progressive-disclosure)|Learning sciences (Ausubel, Reigeluth)|Understanding improves when complexity is introduced gradually — global architecture first, then local elaboration — rather than presenting local detail before the audience can see its role.|

### VIII — Dialectical and Contextual Norms

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Resolution criterion**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^resolution-criterion)|Argumentation theory (van Eemeren & Grootendorst)|An argumentative discourse is a procedure for resolving a determinate rational disagreement; every sub-argument must contribute toward resolving that specific disagreement.|
|[**Context sensitivity**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^context-sensitivity)|Argumentation theory (van Eemeren)|Standards of adequacy depend on the activity type; different genres — scientific explanation, mathematical proof, legal argument, pedagogical exposition — impose distinct dialectical norms.|
|[**Fair reconstruction**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^fair-reconstruction)|Informal logic (Quine, Davidson)|An argument should be evaluated in its strongest reasonable form; accidental ambiguities must be eliminated before rigorous assessment begins.|
|[**Audience adaptation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^audience-adaptation)|Functional rhetoric (Perelman & Olbrechts-Tyteca)|A discourse must be adjusted to the conceptual state of its audience without sacrificing inferential integrity; vocabulary, granularity, and pacing must be tuned accordingly.|
|[**Intellectual virtues**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^intellectual-virtues)|Epistemology of knowledge (Sosa, Zagzebski, Code)|Reliable inquiry requires humility, conscientiousness, open-mindedness, fairness to alternatives, intellectual courage, and precision; uncertainty must be represented honestly.|
|[**Avoidance of fallacious shortcuts**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^avoidance-fallacious-shortcuts)|Informal logic (Woods & Walton, Hamblin)|A discourse must not simulate justification without actually providing it; dependence on equivocation, circularity, irrelevant appeal, hasty generalization, or false dichotomy must be detected and eliminated.|

### IX — Cognitive Architecture and Processing

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Mental model construction**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^mental-model-construction)|Cognitive science (Johnson-Laird)|A discourse is cognitively powerful when it enables the construction of an internal model that can be manipulated rather than merely memorized; it should be organized around relations and mechanisms that permit mental simulation.|
|[**Chunking and compression**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^chunking-compression)|Cognitive science (Miller, Chase & Simon, Chater & Vitányi)|Human cognition handles structured chunks better than long flat sequences; many local facts should be compressed into a few organized units with intermediate landmarks.|
|[**Cognitive economy**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^cognitive-economy)|Cognitive science (Simon, Sweller, Kahneman)|A discourse should minimize unnecessary processing burden — irrelevant detours, notational overload, long dependencies — while preserving essential structure.|
|[**Causal and structural preference**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^causal-structural-preference)|Cognitive science (Sloman, Gopnik & Wellman, Carey)|Human cognition has a strong affinity for causal, mechanistic, and dependency-based organization; such organization supports deeper retention and transfer than disconnected proposition lists.|
|[**Analogical support**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^analogical-support)|Cognitive science (Gentner)|Understanding is strengthened by mapping unfamiliar structure onto familiar structure, provided the analogy preserves the relevant relations (structure-mapping) rather than surface resemblance; scope and limits must always be marked.|
|[**Contrastive explanation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^contrastive-explanation)|Cognitive science and philosophy of science (Lipton, van Fraassen, Hilton & Slugoski)|Understanding is sharpened when a result is shown to hold instead of a nearby plausible alternative; contrasts exclude alternatives and sharpen relevance.|
|[**Retrieval and generativity**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^retrieval-generativity)|Cognitive science (Wittrock, Roediger & Karpicke)|A discourse is cognitively stronger when it enables the audience to regenerate the result rather than merely recognize it; strategies must be made explicit and intermediate questions formulated.|

### X — Knowledge Construction and Transfer

|Principle|Source field|Core content|
|---|---|---|
|[**Prior knowledge anchoring**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^prior-knowledge-anchoring)|Learning sciences (Ausubel)|New ideas must be connected to existing conceptual structures; prerequisite knowledge must be explicitly identified and reactivated before new reasoning is introduced.|
|[**Conceptual sequencing**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^conceptual-sequencing)|Learning sciences (Gagné, Reigeluth)|Concepts must be introduced according to dependency and intelligibility — not formal taxonomy — with motivating tensions preceding abstract resolution and foundational distinctions placed early.|
|[**Scaffolding**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^scaffolding)|Learning sciences (Vygotsky, Wood, Bruner & Ross)|Complex reasoning must be supported by temporary structures that make demanding transitions manageable; support is progressively removed once the pattern is internalized.|
|[**Worked-example structure**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^worked-example-structure)|Learning sciences (Sweller, Atkinson, Renkl)|Powerful explanations alternate between general principle and carefully chosen example; the example instantiates the principle and is followed by abstraction of the transferable pattern.|
|[**Self-explanation inducement**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^self-explanation-inducement)|Learning sciences (Chi et al.)|Understanding increases when the audience must actively reconstruct why a step is valid; inferential purposes must be signaled and the necessity of each step exposed.|
|[**Managing misconceptions**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^managing-misconceptions)|Learning sciences (Posner, Vosniadou, diSessa)|A discourse must explicitly identify and correct likely misunderstandings; the correct structure must be contrasted with the tempting incorrect one at points of systematic ambiguity.|
|[**Transfer orientation**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^transfer-orientation)|Learning sciences (Perkins & Salomon, Bransford & Schwartz)|A strong explanation must explicitly state the reusable schema, identify the invariant structure across instances, and distinguish surface details from the deep generative pattern.|
|[**Feedback-sensitive structure**](principles-reasoning-argumentation-by-field.md#^feedback-sensitive-structure)|Learning sciences (Black & Wiliam, Sadler)|A discourse should include checkpoints enabling diagnosis of whether the intended structure has been genuinely grasped, distinguishing recognition from genuine reconstruction.|
