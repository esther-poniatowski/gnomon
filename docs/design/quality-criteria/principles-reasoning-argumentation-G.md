
# Principles of reasoning and argumentation - Cross-domain taxonomy

## Global classification of principles

| Family                                          | Functional question                                                                                                                           | Distinctive contribution                                                                                                        |
| ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Structural transparency**                     | Is each step locally interpretable, globally situated, and explicitly licensed — with no concealed inferential jumps?                         | Separates merely formally valid discourse from discourse whose structure can actually be inspected, tracked, and reconstructed. |
| **Inferential relevance and economy**           | Does each element perform genuine inferential or explanatory work, without redundancy or unnecessary complexity?                              | Two sub-norms: local relevance (why this step matters here) and global economy (why this route over a needlessly indirect one). |
| **Justification strength and calibration**      | Are claims supported with no more authority than the support warrants — evaluated for strength, independence, stability, and defeasibility?   | Separates structurally explicit reasoning from reasoning whose epistemic force is actually well measured.                       |
| **Dialectical robustness**                      | Can the reasoning be challenged, defended, tracked under rational opposition?                                                                 | Separates arguments convincing in monologue from arguments stable under interactive scrutiny.                                   |
| **Explanatory structure**                       | Does the discourse reveal what generates, determines, or differentiates the result — beyond merely showing the conclusion follows?            | Three subtypes: causal/mechanistic, structural/nomological, and symmetry-/invariance-based.                                     |
| **Systemic coherence and integration**          | Do the claims form an intelligible network of dependence, coherence, and theoretical placement?                                               | Separates a sequence of valid local arguments from a body of reasoning that yields a stable conceptual picture.                 |
| **Understanding, intelligibility and transfer** | Does the discourse support manipulation, variation, reuse, and extension?                                                                     | Addresses the distinction between explanation and understanding.                                                                |
| **Pedagogical exposition**                      | Is the presentation sequenced and framed so that inferential and explanatory structure becomes cognitively available — without loss of rigor? | Norms governing not only what reasoning is correct, but how it should be introduced, paced, and supported.                      |
| **Epistemic conduct**                           | Does the inquiry maintains discipline in handling alternatives, uncertainty, objections, and one's own degree of support?                     | Concerns the attitude of the inquirer, not the inferential architecture of the argument.                                        |

## Overlaps and distinctions

Some principles are close, but should not be merged prematurely.

1. Relevance has several distinct meanings:
	- **logical/inferential relevance**: the premise is actually used
	- **probabilistic relevance**: the premise changes credence
	- **explanatory relevance**: the factor makes a difference to the phenomenon
	- **dialectical relevance**: the move contributes to resolving the dispute

2. Transparency, explanation, and understanding are distinct. A discourse may be:
	- **transparent but non-explanatory**
	- **explanatory but still opaque**
	- **transparent and explanatory but still not understanding-producing**

3. Sufficiency and robustness are distinct. A set of premises may be sufficient in a narrow sense and yet:
	- fragile under prior variation
	- defeated by correlated evidence
	- unable to answer objections
	- unusable for transfer

4. Unification and economy are related but not equivalent:
	- **Economy** reduces unnecessary load
	- **Unification** reveals that many cases instantiate one deeper structure

5. Appropriate abstraction is a balance principle. This principle is irreducible. It mediates between two symmetrical failures:
	- too much low-level detail, producing overload
	- too much abstraction, erasing the real dependency structure
