
# Principles of reasoning and argumentation - By field

## Objective

The question concerns a form of discourse that satisfies **two distinct criteria**:

1. **Epistemic validity**: the discourse is a succession of *correct inferential steps*, that are each reliable, well-justified, resistant to error, and normatively sound.

2. **Cognitive efficacy**: the discourse produces *understanding*, so that the audience grasps _why_ a conclusion holds, with the attendant capacity to generalize, transfer, and act.

> [!QUESTION]
> What principles transform a succession of valid claims into a structured discourse, that yields insight, intelligibility, and transferable understanding?

A succession of valid claims corresponds to a _proof object_ in the logician's sense; understanding is closer to a _proof term with its reduction behavior_ — a structure that reveals _why_ the reduction succeeds.

## Question decomposition

The question can be reformulated as three research questions:

| Question             | Focus                                                                                                                  | Relevant fields (see below)                                                |
| -------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Normative question   | What makes a reasoning justified, reliable, and robust (structure and evaluation of discourse)                         | - logic<br>- epistemology<br>- argumentation theory                        |
| Explanatory question | What makes a reasoning reveal why or how a proposition holds (nature of understanding)                                 | - philosophy of science<br>- theories of explanation                       |
| Cognitive question   | What makes a reasoning easy to internalize, manipulate, and reuse (explanatory structures that are mentally effective) | - cognitive science <br>- learning sciences<br>- psychology of explanation |

## Main research fields

The most fertile area is the zone where **justification, explanation, and cognition** are studied together.

| Field                                                                                                                                                  | Focus                                                                                      | Concepts                                                                                                                                                                                                                                |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Proof theory** (Normalization, Structural proof theory, Complexity, Relevance logic)                                                                 | What is the _form_ of deductions, _independently of their semantic content_?               | - subformula property, cut-elimination<br>- proof-theoretic semantics, meaning grounded in introduction rules<br>- proof complexity, _size_ of proofs as a measure of their intrinsic difficulty                                        |
| **Bayesian epistemology & Probabilistic argumentation**                                                                                                | How to reason under *uncertainty*?                                                         | - coherence-based framework: beliefs form a probability distribution<br>- arguments as _update mechanisms_                                                                                                                              |
| **Dialogical logic & Game-theoretic semantics**                                                                                                        |                                                                                            | - dialogical logic, winning strategy<br>- procedural justification<br>- game-theoretic                                                                                                                                                  |
| **Argumentation theory & Pragma-dialectics**                                                                                                           | How to give and evaluate *reasons* <br>(beyond formal validity)?                           | - distinction between persuasion, explanation, proof<br>- structure of arguments, inference schemes  <br>- justification and defeasibility                                                                                              |
| **Informal logic** (often included within argumentation theory)                                                                                        | What are the standards of reasoning in ordinary and semi-formal discourse (in _practice_)? | - acceptability, relevance, sufficiency, coherence                                                                                                                                                                                      |
| **Philosophy of mathematic explanation**                                                                                                               | When is a proof _explanatory_, as opposed to merely valid?                                 |                                                                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| **Philosophy of science & explanation**<br>- deductive-nomological models<br>- causal-mechanistic explanation<br>- unificationism<br>- interventionism | How to produce *understanding*?                                                            | - distinction between explanation, description, prediction<br>- causal, unificatory, mechanistic, and interventionist accounts of explanation<br>- how scientific theories organize phenomena into intelligible structures              |
| **Epistemology of knowledge**<br>(+ virtue epistemology)                                                                                               | What norms define knowledge, justification, and rational belief?                           | - role of coherence, dependence structure, explanatory integration<br>- what counts as "good" justification<br>- what intellectual virtues support knowledge                                                                            |
| **Epistemology of understanding**                                                                                                                      | What is _understanding_, and how does it differ from mere propositional knowledge?         | - distinction between *understanding* and propositional knowledge                                                                                                                                                                       |
| **Functional rhetoric of exposition & Communication theory**                                                                                           | How should reasoning be *presented* to be intelligible for an audience?                    | - salient reasons<br>- memorability                                                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Cognitive science of explanation and reasoning**                                                                                                     | How humans actually process explanations and arguments to construct mental models?         | - what kinds of explanations are cognitively satisfying<br>- which discourse structures improve learning and transfer<br>- role of causal models, abstraction, examples, analogies, hierarchical organization, cognitive load, chunking |
| **Learning sciences & Educational psychology**                                                                                                         | How exposition modalities support learning?                                                | - sequencing of concepts<br>- scaffolding<br>- conceptual change<br>- explanatory examples<br>- productive failure<br>- self-explanation<br>- analogical transfer                                                                       |

> [!WARNING] Insufficient field
> *Logic* alone is too narrow, because formal validity does not capture explanatory organization.

## Field-by-field principles

These fields do not optimize exactly the same target: some emphasize **normative correctness**, others **explanatory depth**, others **cognitive tractability**. Their principles must therefore be distinguished and then articulated.

### Proof theory (Normalization, Structural proof theory, Complexity, Relevance logic)

| Principle                                                                                                                   | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                    |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Canonical normal form** (Gentzen, Prawitz) ^canonical-normal-form                                                         | Arguments should be reducible to a _normal form_ in which every step is a direct application of introduction or elimination rules, with no detours. A detour — introducing a connective only to immediately eliminate it — signals a conceptual shortcut that may conceal structure.                                                                           | - eliminate detours to force conceptual clarity                                                                                                             |
| **Detour elimination** (Gentzen) ^detour-elimination                                                                        | In a proof in normal form, every intermediate proposition introduced must be _logically contained_ in either the premises or the conclusion — it must be a subformula, or a semantic component, of what is already in play. This is a structural criterion for _locality_ and _relevance_ — proofs that violate it are in a precise sense indirect and opaque. | - minimize importing external propositions that are then eliminated (cuts)  <br>- when used, explicitly mark them as abbreviations to be eventually reduced |
| **Introduction-rule primacy** (Dag Prawitz) ^introduction-rule-primacy                                                      | The meaning of each concept employed (logical constants) must be grounded in how it is _constructed_ (its introduction conditions), making the inferential role primary. Arguments that use a concept without grounding it in its introduction conditions (e.g., proofs by contradiction that produce no canonical witness) are cognitively less transparent.  | - define before using                                                                                                                                       |
| **Relevance of premises** (Gentzen, Anderson & Belnap, relevance logic / substructural proof theory) ^relevance-of-premises | Every premise must be _actually used_ in deriving the conclusion. Unused premises signal either redundancy (benign but inefficient) or concealed load-bearing (the argument implicitly depends on what was declared irrelevant). Structural rule of _weakening_ in sequent calculus: a system that blocks weakening enforces premise use.                      |                                                                                                                                                             |
| **Complexity audit** (Cook & Reckhow, Kreisel) ^complexity-audit                                                            | Disproportionate proof size relative to the conclusion is evidence of an indirect route — and indirect routes typically suppress structure that a more direct argument would reveal.                                                                                                                                                                           | - assess the _inferential length_ and _branching depth_ of an argument                                                                                      |

### Bayesian epistemology & Probabilistic argumentation

| Principle                                                             | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Evidential relevance as probabilistic dependence** (Haenni, Hunter) ^evidential-relevance | A premise is _genuinely relevant_ to a conclusion if and only if it induces a non-trivial update: P(C \mid E) \neq P(C). An argument is cognitively efficient to the extent that it produces large shifts in credence with minimal evidential input. | - check whether verifying each proposition constitutes genuine evidence for the next                                                                                |
| **Proportionality of credence shift** (Joyce, van Fraassen) ^proportionality-credence-shift | The degree of confidence attached to a conclusion must be proportional to the cumulative evidential support provided.                                                                                                                                | - avoid jumping from moderate evidence to strong conclusions                                                                                                        |
| **Prior sensitivity analysis** (Levi, Berger, Hunter) ^prior-sensitivity-analysis | An argument must robust across a _range of reasonable priors_. If the conclusion is sensitive to the exact choice of prior in a domain where priors are contested, this sensitivity must be acknowledged explicitly.                                 |                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Independence of evidence** (Bovens & Hartmann) ^independence-of-evidence | When multiple pieces of evidence are assembled, their _conditional independence_ (given the hypothesis) must be examined. Evidence that is correlated — sharing a common cause — provides less cumulative support.                                   |                                                                                                                                                                     |
| **Calibrating confidence** (Joyce, Williamson) ^calibrating-confidence | Proportioning commitment to evidence: weak evidence licenses limited commitment; strong evidence licenses stronger commitment.                                                                                                                       | - modulate language accordingly  <br>- do not suppress residual uncertainty where it matters  <br>- keep inferential confidence proportional to the evidential base |

### Dialogical logic & Game-theoretic semantics

| Principle                                                                                                | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                      | Operational consequences                                                     |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Reconstructibility by an adversary** / **Procedural transparency** (Lorenzen & Lorenz, Barth & Krabbe) ^reconstructibility-adversary | An argument must be formulated such that a rational and informed _opponent_ could reconstruct every move in the argumentative game.                                                                                          | - avoid implicit steps, unexplained leaps                                    |
| **Strategic completeness** (Lorenzen–Lorenz, Hintikka) ^strategic-completeness | The argument must constitute a _complete winning strategy_ — not merely a sequence of locally correct moves. Each step must be chosen with awareness of how an opponent could challenge it and what responses are available. |                                                                              |
| **Incremental semantic construction** ^incremental-semantic-construction | Understanding is constructed _step by step_ through the dialogue; each move must carry _identifiable semantic content_. Semantic values are constructed move by move through evaluation games.                               | - avoid large leaps whose intermediate states carry no interpretable meaning |
| **Quantifier and scope explicitness** (Hintikka) ^quantifier-scope-explicitness | The _order of quantifier choices_, conditionals, and presuppositions is semantically critical (who chooses what, when). Ambiguities in quantifier scope routinely generate fallacies of apparent generality.                 | - make scope dependencies and the order of contextual choices fully explicit |
| **Concession tracking** (Barth & Krabbe, Hamblin, Walton & Krabbe) ^concession-tracking | Maintain an explicit record of what has been conceded by each party at each stage.                                                                                                                                           | - avoid implicitly conceded propositions without acknowledging them          |

### Argumentation theory & Pragma-dialectics

| Principle                                                                 | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Explicit inferential structure** (Toulmin) ^explicit-inferential-structure | A strong argument must display its *inferential articulation*, i.e. the *pattern of dependence*. Arguments decompose into distinct components that carry distinct _epistemic loads_ (e.g. _data_, _warrant_, _backing_, _qualifier_, _rebuttal_, _claim_). Every inferential step must expose its _warrant_ — the general rule or principle that licenses the move from data to claim. An argument that suppresses its warrant forces the interlocutor to reconstruct it implicitly. | - separate data, warrants, and conclusion<br>- state whether a step is deductive, abductive, analogical, causal, defeasible, or probabilistic<br>- expose the strategy of the argument                          |
| **Backing specification** (Toulmin) ^backing-specification | The warrant itself requires _backing_: the domain-specific body of knowledge or methodology that justifies the warrant's applicability.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              |                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Rebuttal pre-emption** (Toulmin) ^rebuttal-pre-emption | Every argument must explicitly map its _rebuttal space_ — conditions under which the inference fails or the claim does not hold.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Distinction of dialectical roles** (van Eemeren & Grootendorst, Walton) ^distinction-dialectical-roles | Argumentative discourse decomposes into distinct speech acts governed by procedural rules: _advancing a standpoint_, _challenging_, _defending_, _conceding_, _withdrawing a claim_. Each stage of a reasoning sequence must have a clear role.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      |                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Resolution criterion** (van Eemeren & Grootendorst) ^resolution-criterion | An argumentative discourse is a _procedure_ for _resolving rational disagreement_, it must be anchored to a _determinate difference of opinion_. Every sub-argument must contribute toward resolving that specific disagreement.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     |                                                                                                                                                                                                                 |
| **Dialectical robustness** (van Eemeren & Grootendorst) ^dialectical-robustness | A robust argument must be capable of *handling objections* and *withstanding challenge*. Robustness requires to treat objections, alternatives, counterexamples, and defeaters.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      | - identify the strongest plausible objection<br>- delimit the scope of the claim<br>- specify under which conditions the reasoning would fail<br>- distinguish decisive support from merely prima facie support |
| **Burden-of-proof discipline** (Walton) ^burden-of-proof-discipline | Different claims impose different justificatory demands. Strong, surprising, universal, or normatively loaded claims require stronger support.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | - calibrate the depth of justification to the strength of the claim<br>- avoid presenting conjectural claims in the same discursive register as established ones                                                |
| **Context sensitivity** (Van Eemeren) ^context-sensitivity | The standards of adequacy depend partly on *context*: different activity types impose distinct dialectical norms and inferential forms (e.g. for scientific explanation, mathematical proof, legal argument, pedagogical exposition, policy recommendation...).                                                                                                                                                                                                                      | - define the discourse genre  <br>- evaluate reasoning against the norms of that genre  <br>- do not confuse standards across contexts                                                                          |

**Limitation**: It does not always explain what makes a discourse deeply illuminating rather than merely _defensible_.

### Informal logic

The triad **relevance, sufficiency, acceptability** (Johnson & Blair) is one of the most practical evaluation frameworks.

| Principle                                                                           | Content                                                                                                                                                                                      | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Relevance of premises to the conclusion** (Johnson & Blair) ^relevance-premises-conclusion | The premises must be *genuinely pertinent* to the conclusion.                                                                                                                                | - for every step, answer the question: _why does this matter for the target claim?_<br>- avoid ornamental lemmas, detached facts, or technically correct but strategically inert remarks                                                   |
| **Sufficiency of support** (Johnson & Blair) ^sufficiency-of-support | The premises must provide *enough support*. This principle guards against discourses that are suggestive but under-justified (e.g. inferential jump without the needed intermediate bridge). | - identify missing intermediate claims<br>- make explicit the inferential scheme linking premises to conclusion<br>- test whether an informed critic could reasonably deny the conclusion while accepting the premises                     |
| **Acceptability or legitimacy of premises** (Johnson & Blair) ^acceptability-premises | The premises must be *entitled to function as support*. A robust reasoning cannot rests on dubious, undefended, equivocal, or illicit assumptions.                                           | - distinguish granted premises, previously established results, definitions, hypotheses, and substantive assumptions<br>- mark the status of each premise explicitly<br>- prevent hidden assumptions from doing essential inferential work |
| **Avoidance of fallacious shortcuts** (fallacy theory, Woods & Walton, Hamblin) ^avoidance-fallacious-shortcuts | A discourse must not _simulate_ justification without actually providing it                                                                                                                  | test whether the argument depends on typical patterns: equivocation, circularity, irrelevant appeal, hasty generalization, straw man, false dichotomy...                                                                                   |
| **Fair reconstruction** (Quine, Davidson, import from philosophy of interpretation) ^fair-reconstruction | An argument should be evaluated in its strongest reasonable form, not in a weakened caricature.                                                                                              | - reconstruct the intended best version before criticizing  <br>- eliminate accidental ambiguities that are not essential  <br>- then evaluate the strengthened form rigorously                                                            |

### Philosophy of mathematical explanation

| Principle                                                      | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                           |
| -------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Local–global articulation** (Mancosu, Grothendieck) ^local-global-articulation | The argument must distinguish proof and theory-building. Certain arguments establish _why this particular conclusion holds_ (local understanding), while others situate the conclusion within a _broader theoretical architecture_ (global understanding).                                                                                                        |                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Unification** (Kitcher, Friedman) ^unification | Understanding grows when many phenomena can be derived from a small set of principles, patterns (_argument schemata_) or independently assumed primitives. The gain is not only economy, it is **structural compression**: disparate facts become instances of a common architecture.                                                                             | - search for common generating principles<br>- group results by common mechanism rather than by superficial topic<br>- prefer frameworks that reduce fragmentation |
| **Characteristic property dependence** (Steiner) ^characteristic-property-dependence | An explanatory proof must identify a _characteristic / essential property_ of the central entity involved, that makes the conclusion possible. The argument must then be structured so that this property does the critical work — and structured so that varying the property demonstrably varies the conclusion. Non-explanatory proofs succeed "accidentally." | - does the argument still succeed if the characteristic property of the central object is varied?                                                                  |
| **Explanatory asymmetry** (Lange) ^explanatory-asymmetry-math | Some proofs reveal _because_ relations rather than mere material conditionals. Symmetry-based proofs (exploiting invariance) are paradigm cases.                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Symmetry and invariance exploitation** (Lange, Van Fraassen) ^symmetry-invariance-exploitation | When a conclusion holds because of an underlying symmetry or invariance, the argument must make this explicit. They are paradigm cases of explanatory asymmetry.                                                                                                                                                                                                  |                                                                                                                                                                    |
| **Computational affordances** (Avigad) ^computational-affordances | Understanding a proof provides inferential affordances beyond its mere conclusion: navigating proofs, extracting structure, recognizing methods, and supporting further inference (rather than merely certifying truth).                                                                                                                                          |                                                                                                                                                                    |


### Philosophy of science & explanation

> [!NOTE] This table provides a synthesis across distinct theories and traditions.

| Principle                                                                              | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                                                                  |
| -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Explanatory relevance** (Salmon, Woodward) ^explanatory-relevance | Explanation requires selecting factors that make a *difference* to the phenomenon.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             | - distinguish background conditions from explanatory drivers<br>- ask which factors, if varied, would modify the phenomenon<br>- prioritize determinants over accompanying facts                          |
| **Why- and How-questions** (Bromberger, Fraassen) ^why-how-questions | A discourse produces understanding when it is organized around a precise explanatory target:<br>- *why* does the phenomenon occur?<br>- *how* is it generated?<br>- *under what structure or mechanism* does it become necessary or expected?<br>The "why" question is relative to a contrast class, a relevance relation, and a body of background knowledge. | - define the exact explanatory question before constructing the discourse<br>- avoid mixing description, classification, proof, and explanation without signaling the shift                               |
| **Causal or mechanistic articulation** (Salmon, Machamer, Darden, Craver, Woodward) ^causal-mechanistic-articulation | Understanding is enhanced when the discourse identifies the *process*, *mechanism*, or *causal* organization that produces the result.                                                                                                                                                                                                                         | - identify entities, relations, operations, and their organization<br>- explain _how_ the underlying system generates the result<br>- in abstract domains, provide structural generation when appropriate |
| **Counterfactual depth** (manipulationist / interventionist accounts, Lewis, Woodward) ^counterfactual-depth | Understanding is not passive recognition; it is the ability to mentally manipulate the structure. A powerful explanation supports answers to *counterfactual questions*:<br>- what would happen if this factor were removed?<br>- which variations preserve the result?<br>- which perturbations destroy it?<br>                                               | - include perturbation analysis<br>- state necessary versus contingent features<br>- show invariants and dependence relations                                                                             |
| **Necessity structure** (Hempel & Oppenheim's Deductive-Nomological (DN) model) ^necessity-structure | A discourse produces understanding when it reveals why a phenomenon is nomologically *expected* or *derivable* under laws and antecedent conditions.                                                                                                                                                                                                           | - distinguish accidental regularities from consequences of structure<br>- make constraints explicit<br>- show how the conclusion emerges from the architecture of the system                              |
| **Explanatory asymmetry** (Bromberger, Salmon) ^explanatory-asymmetry-science | The direction of the argument must respect the underlying _because_ relation (real explanatory priority). Two statements may be logically equivalent, yet one explains the other and not conversely.                                                                                                                                                           | - organize the discourse according to generative direction <br>- identify and respect the asymmetric dependency structure of the domain                                                                   |

### Epistemology of knowledge

| Principle                                                                            | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                          | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                        |
| ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Justification tracking** (Nozick, Sosa, Williamson) ^justification-tracking | A claim should not appear in discourse with more epistemic authority than its support warrants.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  | - calibrate modal strength: possible, plausible, supported, established, necessary<br>- avoid overclaiming<br>- distinguish theorem, conjecture, heuristic, and interpretation                                                                                                                                  |
| **Coherence of the belief system** (BonJour, Lehrer) ^coherence-belief-system | The claims should form a *coherent network* rather than a set of disconnected commitments.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                       | - test compatibility among claims<br>- ensure that local arguments integrate into a globally stable picture<br>- track whether later claims undermine earlier assumptions                                                                                                                                       |
| **Sensitivity to defeaters** (Pollock, defeasibility theory, Reiter, Hunter & Thimm) ^sensitivity-to-defeaters | A belief may be justified until a *defeating consideration* appears. A cognitively honest argument emphasizes conditions under which support is cancelled or weakened. It specifies the _threshold of defeating evidence_ at which the conclusion should be abandoned, and presents the current state as occupying a position on that continuum. | - explicitly record known defeaters<br>- identify empirical, conceptual, or methodological conditions that would force revision<br>- assess how the argument's conclusion degrades when defeaters are introduced (rebutting or undercutting evidence)<br>- treat fragile arguments differently from stable ones |
| **Intellectual virtues** (Sosa, Zagzebski, Code) ^intellectual-virtues | Virtue epistemology emphasizes traits that make inquiry reliable and understanding-oriented:<br>- intellectual humility<br>- conscientiousness<br>- open-mindedness<br>- fairness to alternatives<br>- intellectual courage<br>- precision                                                                                                       | - represent uncertainty honestly<br>- consider alternative explanations<br>- avoid premature closure<br>- state limits and unresolved tensions                                                                                                                                                                  |

### Epistemology of understanding

> [!NOTE] This field is concerned by the distinction between knwoledge (explanation) and *understanding*.

| Principle                                                                   | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                 |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Coherence integration** (Kvanvig) ^coherence-integration | Each claim must be explicitly connected to others through _coherence relations_ — explanatory, inferential, contrastive, or evidential.                                                                                                                                                   |                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Grasp of dependence relations** (Kvanvig, Grimm) ^grasp-dependence-relations | Understanding consists precisely in grasping dependenceand modal relations among facts (objectual understanding). ==TODO: clarify==                                                                                                                                                       | - ask whether the set of claims reveals an organized dependence structure                                                                                |
| **Counterfactual robustness** (Grimm, Zagzebski, de Regt) ^counterfactual-robustness | An argument must support _what-if-things-had-been-different_ reasoning, i.e. enable to identify which modifications to premises or conditions would alter the conclusion, and _how_.                                                                                                      |                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Intelligibility criterion** (de Regt) ^intelligibility-criterion | An argument must enables recognition of _qualitatively correct behavior_ without full re-derivation. Concretely, it should enable to predict the direction of variation of the conclusion under perturbation or answer novel questions without re-execution from scratch. |                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Minimization of epistemic opacity** (Avigad, de Regt) ^minimization-epistemic-opacity | Each inferential step must be individually scrutable. If a step requires importing an unanalyzed black-box result, that result must either be analyzed in place or explicitly flagged as an assumed module with its own justification deferred.                           |                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Ability to transfer, reorganize and extend knowledge** (Zagzebski, Hills) ^transfer-reorganize-extend | Understanding-why differs from knowing-why by the ability to answer a range of _wh_-questions from a position of insight.                                                                                                                                                 |                                                                                                                                                          |
| **Appropriate abstraction** (Kvanvig, Grimm) ^appropriate-abstraction | An explanation should not drown the audience in low-level detail if a higher-level organization captures the decisive pattern. Conversely, it should not remain so abstract that the real generating structure disappears.                                                | - choose the level of abstraction at which the dependencies become intelligible<br>- remove inessential detail while preserving explanatory determinants |


### Functional rhetoric of exposition & Communication theory

| Principle                                             | Content                                                                                                                                         | Operational consequences                                                                                                                              |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Audience adaptation** (Perelman & Olbrechts-Tyteca) ^audience-adaptation | A discourse must be adjusted to the conceptual state of its audience, but not by sacrificing rigor.                                             | - tune vocabulary, granularity, and pacing to the audience  <br>- preserve inferential integrity while changing exposition                            |
| **Salience hierarchy** (Perelman) ^salience-hierarchy | A discourse must mark hierarchical emphasis: main idea, decisive step, subordinate detail, optional remark.                                     | - visibly distinguish backbone from support  <br>- control emphasis and progression  <br>- prevent peripheral details from masking the core structure |
| **Framing and orientation** (Goffman, Entman) ^framing-orientation | A discourse becomes more intelligible when the audience knows from the start what problem is being solved and why the proposed route is chosen. | - state the target question  <br>- announce the strategy  <br>- indicate the role of each section in the whole                                        |

### Cognitive science of reasoning and explanation

| Principle                                                              | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                      |
| ---------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Mental model construction** (Johnson-Laird) ^mental-model-construction | A discourse is cognitively powerful when it enables the construction of an internal model that can be *manipulated*, not merely memorized.                                                                                                | - organize reasoning around relations, mechanisms, or schemas that can be mentally simulated<br>- prefer structures that permit reconstruction from principle |
| **Chunking and compression** (Miller, Chase & Simon, Chater & Vitányi) ^chunking-compression | Human cognition handles structured chunks better than long flat sequences. Understanding often consists in compressing many local facts into a few organized units.                                                                       | - group steps into conceptual modules<br>- mark intermediate landmarks<br>- replace repeated local derivations by a higher-order principle when legitimate    |
| **Cognitive economy** (Simon, Sweller, Kahneman) ^cognitive-economy | A discourse should minimize unnecessary processing burden while preserving essential structure.                                                                                                                                           | - eliminate irrelevant detours<br>- reduce notation overload<br>- avoid long dependencies where a local summary can stabilize comprehension                   |
| **Causal and structural preference** (Sloman, Gopnik & Wellman, Carey) ^causal-structural-preference | Human cognition has a strong affinity for causal, mechanistic, and dependency-based organization. Such organization tends to support deeper retention and transfer than disconnected proposition lists.                                   | - emphasize generative relations<br>- answer what produces what, what depends on what, and why the structure behaves as it does                               |
| **Analogical support** (Gentner) ^analogical-support | Understanding is often strengthened by mapping an unfamiliar structure to a familiar one, provided the analogy preserves the relevant relations. The structure-mapping theory distinguishes surface similarity from relational alignment. | - use analogies to expose relational structure, not superficial resemblance<br>- always mark the scope and limits of the analogy                              |
| **Contrastive explanation** (Lipton, van Fraassen, Hilton & Slugoski) ^contrastive-explanation | A mind often understands better when shown why a result holds *instead of* a nearby alternative.                                                                                                                                          | - compare the actual result with the most plausible alternative<br>- explain what excludes the alternative<br>- use contrasts to sharpen relevance            |
| **Retrieval and generativity** (Wittrock, Roediger & Karpicke) ^retrieval-generativity | A discourse is cognitively stronger when it enables the audience to regenerate the result rather than merely recognize it.                                                                                                                | - make strategies explicit<br>- present reusable procedures<br>- formulate intermediate questions that could reproduce the line of thought                    |

### Learning sciences & Educational psychology

| Principle                                                                                           | Content                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                             | Operational consequences                                                                                                                                                                                       |
| --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Prior knowledge anchoring** (Ausubel) ^prior-knowledge-anchoring | A discourse becomes understandable when new ideas are *connected to existing conceptual structures*. A higher-order abstraction must ne presented before new material.                                                                                                                                                                              | - identify prerequisites explicitly<br>- reactivate the conceptual basis on which the new reasoning will rest<br>- avoid using unprepared notions as silent supports                                           |
| **Progressive disclosure** (Ausubel, Reigeluth) ^progressive-disclosure | Understanding improves when complexity is introduced gradually: global architecture first, then local elaboration. (*In contrast, many technically valid reasonings present local details before the audience can see their role*.) | **Conceptual sequencing** (Gagné, Reigeluth) ^conceptual-sequencing | Understanding depends strongly on the *order in which notions are introduced*.                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                      | - present concepts according to dependency and intelligibility, not only according to formal taxonomy<br>- introduce motivating tensions before abstract resolution<br>- place foundational distinctions early |
